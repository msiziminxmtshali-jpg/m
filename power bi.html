Maji Ndongo(national water survey)

This analysis of the Maji Ndongo water crisis uses national survey data to uncover the relationship between infrastructure gaps, health risks, and national security. The findings below highlight how water access directly impacts the safety of women and children.

1. Water Access and Population Distribution

Current Access Levels: The national survey indicates that only 34% of the population has basic water access, while 66% falls below basic standards.

  • Population Split: The country has a total implied population of around 71M (69% Rural vs. 31% Urban). Rural areas dominate, exacerbating access issues due to dispersed infrastructure.
  • Source Usage: Shared taps serve the largest portion (43.84%), followed by tap-in-home (18.32%), wells (14.77%), broken taps (14.55%), and rivers (8.52%).
  • Provincial Variations: Akatsi has the highest population, followed by Sokoto and Kilimani. Rural populations outnumber urban in most provinces.

Insight: The dominance of rural populations and shared sources points to a need for targeted rural infrastructure. The 14.55% of "broken taps" represents a quick-win opportunity for repairs.

2. Queue Management Daily and Weekly Patterns

Patterns: Average queue time peaks on Saturdays (~250-300 min) and Sundays (~150 min), dropping mid-week (Wednesday ~50 min).

  • Hourly Trends: Queues build from morning peaks, dip midday, and rise again in the evening (14:00–20:00).
  • Provincial Totals: Kilimani has the highest total queued time (~1.0M min), followed by Akatsi and Sokoto.
  • Queue vs. Service: Data shows queue time increases non-linearly with people served, suggesting severe overcrowding at high-volume sources.

Insight: Long weekend queues indicate limited access points. Recommendations include extending operating hours and adding public taps in high-queue provinces like Kilimani.

3. Queue Composition & Risk

Demographics: Queues are predominantly female (69%) and children (8%), with males making up only 23%. Women and children bear the main burden of water collection.

  • Daily Patterns: Longest queues occur on Saturdays.
  • Hourly Trends: Queues rise sharply in the late afternoon/evening.
  • Provincial Impact: High queue times in Kilimani force women to wait for hours.

Key Insight: Long weekend and evening queues force women and children to wait or travel during high-risk hours, directly linking to the elevated evening female victimization seen in crime data (harassment/sexual assault spike 18:00–20:00).

4. Pollution Analysis

Contamination Levels: Nearly 100% of recorded pollutants are chemical-based (39.44K ppm), with negligible biological results. This indicates severe industrial or agricultural runoff.

  • Results Breakdown: Clean sources account for 30.92%, Chemical contamination 40.8%, and Biological 28.28%.
  • Provincial Map: Most provinces are marked red (high pollution), implying widespread chemical exposure.

Insight: Chemical pollution is the primary threat. Simple boiling is insufficient; the project must prioritize filtration systems (RO/UV) in red zones to mitigate health impacts.

5. Victim Gender Breakdown

Victims: Females are the majority victims (64%, ~50K cases), followed by males (25%) and children (10%).

  • Top Crimes: Harassment (highest, mostly female victims) and Sexual Assault (almost exclusively female).
  • Highest Risk Provinces: Kilimani and Akatsi have the most victims, correlating with high population and long water queues.
  • Timing: Female victimization spikes in the evening (18:00–20:00).

Key Insight: Inadequate water access forces women and girls to travel long distances at risky hours, significantly increasing vulnerability to harassment and sexual assault.

6. Infrastructure Improvements and Budget

Strategy: Sokoto leads in improvements (~8K), followed by Kilimani (~6K). The primary focus is installing RO filters and repairing infrastructure.

  • Costs: RO Filters and Repairs cost ~100K each. The total budget is $146.74M.
  • Budget Status: Data indicates low current spend ($16K) against the $112M goal, suggesting early-stage implementation or data lag.
  • Access Improvement: Post-improvements, 91% of the population is projected to have basic access—a massive leap from 34%.

Insight: The focus on RO filters is cost-effective for addressing chemical pollution. However, current data suggests execution needs to scale rapidly to meet the 71M population demand.

Key Achievements<

Dramatic increase in basic access (34% to 91%), demonstrating effective interventions. Focus on chemical pollution via RO/UV filters aligns with contamination data. Data-driven insights from queues could inform real-time optimizations. Balanced source distribution, with shared taps providing broad coverage.

Challenges and Recommendations

  • Inequities:Akatsi (high pop, low improvements) and Kilimani (high queues) need priority. Suggestion: Reallocate 20-30% of remaining budget (~146M total) to these areas.
  • Pollution Persistence: Near-100% chemical ppm requires ongoing monitoring and expansion of filtration projects.
  • Low Utilization: 0% completion and low spend suggest administrative hurdles; aim for phased milestones (e.g., 50% by 2026).
  • Queue Inefficiencies:Reduce times by 30-50% through more drill wells in rural areas and app-based queue tracking.
  • Scalability: With 71M population and only 10K helped, target 1M+ beneficiaries by expanding to river-dependent communities (8.52%).
  • Overall Impact: The project has transformed access but must address pollution and equity for sustainability. Future dashboards could include ROI metrics (e.g., cost per person helped) and health outcome correlations. This analysis is derived directly from the visual data in the dashboards. If additional raw data or specific metrics are available, a deeper quantitative review (e.g., via code analysis) could refine these insights.
