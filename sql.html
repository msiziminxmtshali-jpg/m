Project Overview

This project analyzes the Maji Ndongo Water Crisis. Using SQL to clean and investigate national survey data, we uncovered a direct correlation between water access issues and national security threats. Click on the sections below to view the detailed findings.

1: Executive Summary

The Status Quo: 66% of Maji Ndongo citizens lack basic water access.

The Health Risk: Significant chemical contamination found in key provinces.

The Critical Discovery: There is a direct correlation between water queue wait times and national crime rates against women.

The Goal: To utilize the $146M budget to not just provide water, but to improve national security.

2: The Infrastructure Gap

Demographics: Our nation is 69% Rural. The crisis is not happening in the cities, but in the remote provinces.

Access Levels: Only 34% of our population has "Basic Access" (water in the home). The remaining 66% rely on shared taps, wells, or rivers.

The Disparity: As seen in the "Sources by Town" chart, specific towns like Harare and Amina have zero access to in-home taps, relying entirely on shared infrastructure.

Speaker Note: "We are looking at a fundamental infrastructure gap. With the majority of our people living in rural settings, the reliance on 'Shared Taps' and 'Wells' is creating a bottleneck."

3: The Quality Crisis (Pollution)

The Map: The red zones in the dashboard indicate high pollution levels.

The Contaminants: The data is alarming—39,440 samples returned "Contaminated: Chemical." This vastly outweighs biological contamination.

Implication: Simple boiling isn't enough. We require advanced filtration (Reverse Osmosis) infrastructure, not just simple wells.

Speaker Note: "It is not enough to just dig wells. The high prevalence of chemical contamination means we must invest in RO filters, which impacts our budget and timeline."

4: The Human Cost – The "Queue" Analysis

Peak Congestion: The data shows a linear relationship: more people equals exponential wait times.

The Worst Day: Saturday is the critical pain point. Average queue times spike on this day.

The Demographic: Look at the "Composition of Queues." It is almost exclusively Women (Pink) and Children, with very few men present.

Speaker Note: "The burden of fetching water falls disproportionately on women and children. On Saturdays, they are trapped in these queues for hours."

5: The Hidden Crisis – Crime Correlation

Victim Profile: The pie chart is undeniable—the vast majority of crime victims are Female.

Crime Type: The leading crimes are Harassment and Sexual Assault.

The "Golden Hour": Crime peaks between 15:00 and 18:00 (3 PM - 6 PM).

Speaker Note: "This is where the data tells a terrifying story. When we overlay the 'Crime Time' with the 'Queue Time,' we see they overlap. Women are being targeted in the late afternoon while waiting for, or walking home with, water."

6: The Solution – National Improvement Plan

Budget: We have a total budget of $146.74 Million.

Strategy: The plan involves a mix of drilling wells for access and installing RO filters for the chemical pollution.

Focus Areas: Based on the data, we must prioritize Kilimani (highest queues) and provinces with high chemical pollution.

KPI: Our target is to move 91% of the population to "Basic Access."

Speaker Note: "We have the budget. The data dictates that we cannot simply distribute resources evenly; we must target the high-queue, high-crime, and high-pollution zones first to save lives."

7: Recommendations & Conclusion

Immediate Relief: Deploy temporary water tankers to Kilimani on Saturdays to reduce queue times immediately.

Infrastructure Priority: Fast-track RO Filter installation in the chemically contaminated zones shown in the pollution report.

Security: Increase police patrols near shared taps between 15:00 and 18:00, specifically targeting the crime hotspots identified.

Long Term: Drill wells closer to rural homes to eliminate the need for long-distance travel, effectively reducing the "opportunity window" for crime.

Final Statement: "By solving the water crisis, we are not just hydrating a nation; we are protecting its women and children."
